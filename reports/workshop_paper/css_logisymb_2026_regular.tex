\title{Counterfactual Structural Sensitivity for Negation and Argument-Role Consistency}

\author{Anonymous Submission}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We introduce Counterfactual Structural Sensitivity (CSS), a dataset-only protocol for testing how language-model representations react to controlled structural edits. CSS compares each sentence with a counterfactual variant, extracts layer-wise hidden states, and measures shifts with centroid cosine, Frobenius token-matrix similarity, L2 distance, and token-aligned distance. On 3,000 role-reversal and negation pairs, BERT, RoBERTa, and GPT-2 show a clear asymmetry: role-reversal shifts remain positively associated with GPT-2 surprisal after surface-control residualization, while negation effects are weaker and strongly shaped by source-data lexical foils. Frobenius provides small but consistent in-sample value beyond cosine in 70/78 baseline layer/model/phenomenon cells. Modern decoder extensions show near-perfect identity judgments but imperfect counterfactual rejection, especially for negation. CSS is therefore best interpreted as a reproducible diagnostic of structural sensitivity and consistency gaps, not as evidence of human-like language processing.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Small structural edits can change truth conditions while preserving substantial surface overlap. If a sentence is represented as a binary predicate $R(a,b)$, role reversal maps it to $R(b,a)$; unless the relation is symmetric, the two propositions need not be equivalent. A negation edit maps an affirmative proposition toward a polarity contrast. In the data used here, negation often appears together with a category or predicate foil, so we treat it conservatively as a \emph{negated counterfactual} rather than as a pure $\neg P$ transformation.

These edits are relevant to logical and symbolic reasoning because they probe whether models preserve argument-role and polarity distinctions under controlled counterfactuals. Minimal-pair benchmarks have shown that language models capture some grammatical contrasts while failing on others \cite{warstadt2020blimp,ettinger2020bert,shivagunde2023larger}. However, output accuracy alone does not reveal how hidden representations move, and hidden-state probes without counterfactual controls can be dominated by lexical overlap, length, or template artifacts.

We propose Counterfactual Structural Sensitivity (CSS), a protocol that asks whether layer-wise representations move systematically under controlled edits and whether different geometric metrics expose complementary structure. The study is dataset-only: it does not introduce new human annotations and does not claim human-like comprehension. Its primary target is model-internal and output-level sensitivity under public role-reversal and negation data.

The contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A reproducible CSS pipeline for role reversal and negated counterfactuals: pair construction, hidden-state extraction, layer-wise metrics, probes, surprisal, output consistency, and qualitative audit.
    \item A contextual token-matrix Frobenius metric compared directly with centroid cosine, including surface-control residual analyses.
    \item A baseline study over BERT, RoBERTa, and GPT-2, plus modern decoder extensions with Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 and Gemma-3-4B-IT.
    \item A conservative interpretation showing strong role-reversal sensitivity, weaker and more artifact-sensitive negation results, and remaining behavioral consistency gaps in instruction-tuned decoders.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Minimal pairs and argument roles.}
BLiMP uses minimal pairs to evaluate grammatical knowledge \cite{warstadt2020blimp}. Ettinger shows that BERT captures some role and event cues but remains weak on negation \cite{ettinger2020bert}. Lee et al. evaluate language models' sensitivity to argument roles in psycholinguistic settings \cite{lee2024argumentroles}. Shivagunde et al. extend Ettinger-style role-reversal and negation diagnostics to larger datasets \cite{shivagunde2023larger}; we use their public data snapshot as the source for CSS pairs \cite{textmachinelab2026datasetrepo}.

\paragraph{Probing and representation similarity.}
Probing studies ask whether linguistic properties are recoverable from hidden states \cite{conneau2018senteval,tenney2019bert}, but probe accuracy can reflect probe capacity or memorization, motivating control tasks and selectivity \cite{hewitt2019designing}. Our matrix analysis is also related to representational similarity analysis \cite{kriegeskorte2008rsa} and neural representation comparison methods such as CKA \cite{kornblith2019cka}, although CSS is pair-level rather than dataset-geometry-level.

\paragraph{Behavioral consistency and surprisal.}
Controlled behavioral tests such as CheckList and HANS show that aggregate accuracy can hide systematic linguistic failures \cite{ribeiro2020checklist,mccoy2019hans}. CSS complements such tests by connecting behavioral consistency to hidden-state movement. Surprisal provides a probabilistic predictor of processing difficulty \cite{levy2008expectation}; we use GPT-2 left-to-right surprisal \cite{radford2019language}. Masked-LM pseudo-log-likelihood \cite{salazar2020masked} is bidirectional and therefore not used as the primary surprisal construct.

\paragraph{Matrix norms.}
Centroid cosine compresses a sentence to one vector and can discard token-token relational information. vor der Br{\"u}ck and Pouly motivate matrix-norm similarity for document-level embeddings \cite{vonderbruck2019matrix}. CSS adapts that idea to contextual word matrices and tests whether it adds signal beyond centroid cosine.

\section{CSS Protocol}

For a sentence $s$ and counterfactual $s'=T_{\mathrm{edit}}(s)$, CSS extracts hidden states for every layer $l$:
\begin{equation}
    H_l(s) \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}, \quad
    H_l(s') \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times d}.
\end{equation}
Rows are word-level vectors obtained by mean pooling subword pieces; special tokens are excluded. The primary sentence vector is $\bar{h}_l(s)=n^{-1}\sum_i H_l(s)_i$.

\subsection{Consistency Criteria}

For role reversal, the target contrast is predicate-argument permutation: $R(a,b)$ versus $R(b,a)$. The output-level task asks whether the two sentences describe the same event with the same participant roles. For negation, the target contrast is polarity plus predicate/category change as present in the source dataset. The output-level task asks whether the pair preserves the same polarity and predicate meaning. These criteria are weaker than formal theorem proving; they are forced-choice consistency diagnostics.

\subsection{Shift Metrics}

CSS computes four per-layer shifts. Centroid cosine is
\begin{equation}
    \Delta^{\mathrm{cos}}_l =
    1-\cos(\bar{h}_l(s),\bar{h}_l(s')).
\end{equation}
L2 shift is $\Delta^{\mathrm{L2}}_l=\|\bar{h}_l(s)-\bar{h}_l(s')\|_2$.

For Frobenius token-matrix similarity, let $A_l$ and $B_l$ be row-L2-normalized word matrices. Unequal lengths are handled directly: $A_lB_l^\top$ is an $n \times m$ cross-similarity matrix and no token alignment is required. We clip negative cosine similarities with $K(X)=\max(X,0)$ and compute
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{sim}^{\mathrm{frob}}_l =
    \frac{\|K(A_lB_l^\top)\|_F}
    {\sqrt{\|K(A_lA_l^\top)\|_F\|K(B_lB_l^\top)\|_F + \epsilon}},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \Delta^{\mathrm{frob}}_l = 1-\mathrm{sim}^{\mathrm{frob}}_l .
\end{equation}
Token-aligned shift uses exact lowercased word matches and falls back to positional alignment when no exact matches exist:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta^{\mathrm{tok}}_l =
    |\mathcal{A}|^{-1}
    \sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal{A}}
    \left(1-\cos(H_l(s)_i,H_l(s')_j)\right).
\end{equation}

\section{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Data.}
The final dataset contains 1,500 role-reversal pairs from ROLE-1500 and 1,500 negation pairs from NEG-1500-SIMP-GEN \cite{shivagunde2023larger,textmachinelab2026datasetrepo}. We use the public source snapshot at commit \texttt{48cc635}; source SHA-256 hashes are recorded for both imported files. The CSS schema stores sentence pair, phenomenon, edit type, edited spans, split, token lengths, lexical Jaccard, and edit distance. Role examples preserve more lexical material than negation examples; this motivates the surface-control analysis below.

\paragraph{Models.}
The primary baselines are BERT-base-uncased \cite{devlin2019bert}, RoBERTa-base \cite{liu2019roberta}, and GPT-2 \cite{radford2019language}, using embedding layer plus 12 transformer layers. Extension models are Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 \cite{jiang2023mistral,mistralai2024instructv03} and Gemma-3-4B-IT \cite{gemmateam2025gemma3,gemmateam2025gemma3modelcard}, using all hidden layers. Runs use seed 13, evaluation mode, hidden-state output enabled, and cached metadata for model id, package versions, dtype, dataset hash, and configuration hash.

\paragraph{Surprisal.}
GPT-2 token surprisal is $\mathrm{surprisal}(w_i)=-\log p(w_i|w_1,\ldots,w_{i-1})$. We use absolute average-surprisal delta as the main probabilistic covariate. This measures alignment with GPT-2 predictive difficulty, not the larger decoder's own uncertainty.

\paragraph{Probes.}
Linear probes are secondary diagnostics. The role probe classifies span vectors as agent versus patient. The negation probe classifies sentence-mean vectors as affirmative versus negated. Each layer/model/phenomenon uses stratified 80/20 train/test splits, seeds $\{11,13,17,19,23\}$, standardized logistic regression with $C=1.0$, and random-label controls. Selectivity is task macro-F1 minus control macro-F1.

\paragraph{Output consistency.}
For each pair we score the next-token likelihood of the candidates \texttt{ yes} and \texttt{ no}. The prompt is:
\begin{quote}\small
Decide whether two sentences preserve the same meaning. Criterion: They must describe [the same event with the same participant roles / the same polarity and predicate meaning]. Sentence 1: $s$. Sentence 2: $t$. Answer yes or no. Answer:
\end{quote}
Identity controls set $t=s$ and expect yes. Counterfactual trials set $t=s'$ and expect no. Since scoring is forced-choice over two candidate strings, there are no invalid generations.

\paragraph{Statistics.}
We report Spearman correlations between shift metrics and absolute average-surprisal delta, with bootstrap confidence intervals and Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction. A ``cell'' denotes one model, layer, phenomenon, and metric. Because cells reuse the same items across layers, cell counts are descriptive robustness summaries rather than independent evidence units. We additionally compute surface-controlled Spearman correlations by rank-residualizing both the shift metric and surprisal target against lexical Jaccard, absolute length delta, and edit distance. Frobenius incremental value is the in-sample adjusted-$R^2$ gain for target $y=$ absolute average-surprisal delta:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta R^2_{\mathrm{adj}} =
    R^2_{\mathrm{adj}}(y \sim \Delta^{\mathrm{cos}}+\Delta^{\mathrm{frob}})
    - R^2_{\mathrm{adj}}(y \sim \Delta^{\mathrm{cos}}).
\end{equation}

\section{Results}

\subsection{Representation Shifts and Surface Controls}

The strongest result is role reversal. Across BERT, RoBERTa, and GPT-2, all 39 baseline Frobenius role-reversal cells are positive both before and after surface-control residualization. Mean Frobenius Spearman is 0.293 before controls and 0.241 after controls. Negation is weaker: mean Frobenius Spearman is 0.018 before controls and -0.011 after controls, with only 15/39 positive surface-controlled cells. This confirms that negation results should be interpreted as artifact-sensitive source-data behavior rather than a clean polarity-only effect.

The layer profiles support the same asymmetry. For Frobenius shift, role reversal peaks in middle-to-late layers: BERT layer 7 ($\rho=0.329$), RoBERTa layer 7 ($\rho=0.368$), and GPT-2 layer 10 ($\rho=0.318$). Negation peaks early and weakly: BERT layer 0 ($\rho=0.154$), RoBERTa layer 0 ($\rho=0.118$), and GPT-2 layer 0 ($\rho=0.131$).

\subsection{Frobenius Complementarity}

Adding Frobenius beyond cosine gives positive in-sample adjusted-$R^2$ gain in 70/78 baseline cells, with mean gain 0.0114. The effect is modest, so the correct claim is complementarity, not superiority. The same analysis remains mostly positive in modern decoders: 59/66 Mistral cells and 48/70 Gemma cells have positive gain.

\subsection{Probes and Surprisal}

Probe selectivity is consistently above random-label controls, with mean selectivity 0.510 macro-F1. However, selectivity is nearly uncoupled from shift-surprisal alignment: for Frobenius, selectivity versus correlation strength has Spearman $-0.011$ and Pearson $0.013$. Thus recoverability and pair-level movement are complementary diagnostics rather than substitutes.

\subsection{Output-Level Consistency}

Instruction-tuned decoders solve identity controls but not all counterfactuals. Mistral reaches 1.000 identity accuracy for both phenomena, but only 0.734 role-reversal rejection (95\% Wilson CI 0.711--0.756) and 0.652 negation rejection (0.628--0.676). Gemma reaches 0.999 role identity and 1.000 negation identity, with 0.969 role-reversal rejection (0.959--0.976) and 0.814 negation rejection (0.794--0.833). GPT-2 is retained as the surprisal model and biased output baseline because its identity-control accuracy is poor.

\subsection{Modern Decoder Hidden States}

Mistral produces 99,000 metric rows and Gemma produces 105,000 metric rows, with zero Frobenius anomaly warnings. Mistral's strongest Frobenius-surprisal alignment is role reversal at layer 6 ($\rho=0.324$, FDR $q=1.15\times10^{-35}$); Gemma's strongest role-reversal alignment is also layer 6 ($\rho=0.319$, $q=2.25\times10^{-34}$). Negation is weaker: Mistral peaks at layer 0 ($\rho=0.175$) and Gemma at layer 4 ($\rho=0.145$).

\section{Qualitative Analysis}

The qualitative audit explains why mean shift and rank correlation can diverge. Negation has lower lexical overlap and larger length differences than role reversal: mean lexical Jaccard is 0.485 for negation and 0.797 for role reversal; mean absolute length delta is 1.001 for negation and 0.047 for role reversal. Frobenius is also surface-sensitive: mean Spearman correlation between Frobenius shift and lexical Jaccard is -0.325 for negation and -0.446 for role reversal.

Concrete cases make the dissociation visible. A high-shift but low-surprisal negation example is \emph{A hammer is an instrument} versus \emph{A hammer is not a dessert}. The representation moves strongly, but the average surprisal delta is near zero; the example also reveals that the negation data include category foils. A low-shift but high-surprisal role example is \emph{The cashier counted which bills the robber had given} versus \emph{The cashier counted which robber the bills had given}. Surprisal changes sharply while averaged geometry moves less. These cases support a multi-diagnostic interpretation and motivate conservative claims.

\section{Discussion}

CSS separates three questions: whether an edit moves hidden states, whether movement rank-aligns with a probabilistic covariate, and whether an instruction model behaviorally rejects the counterfactual as meaning-preserving. The role-reversal results are the clearest evidence of structural sensitivity: they survive surface controls and show consistent layer profiles across baseline and modern models. Negation is more cautious: the source data probe useful polarity/category contrasts, but not pure logical negation in isolation.

The Frobenius result is useful because token-matrix geometry adds small, repeated in-sample signal beyond centroid cosine. It should not be overclaimed as a universal metric or as direct proof of symbolic reasoning. The output experiment is the main LogiSymb-facing bridge: identity controls are near ceiling for instruction models, yet counterfactual rejection remains imperfect, particularly for negation.

\section{Limitations}

This study has no new human annotations and therefore makes no human-alignment claim. The generated source data contain article mismatches, category-foil substitutions, and implausible role continuations. We do not include null-pair baselines such as random sentence pairs, passive paraphrases, or synonym substitutions; these are important future controls. The yes/no output task is prompt-based and forced-choice. CSS is correlational and does not identify causal mechanisms in hidden states.

\section{Conclusion}

Counterfactual Structural Sensitivity provides a reproducible way to test how language-model representations react to controlled structural edits. The strongest current finding is robust role-reversal sensitivity across layers and models. Negation exposes useful but noisier consistency gaps because source pairs combine polarity with lexical and category changes. Frobenius token-matrix geometry adds modest complementary signal beyond centroid cosine, and modern instruction models still fail on nontrivial fractions of counterfactual consistency judgments.
